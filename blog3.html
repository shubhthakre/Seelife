Children are, and should always be, the heart of any agenda for transformation. Every childâ€™s unrealised potential translates to an unrealised growth potential for the country. It isnâ€™t a surprise that seven out of ten most productive investments stated by the Copenhagen Consensus, one of the biggest thinktanks in the world, are directly related to children.

Our governmentâ€™s intent to positively impact various indicators of child rights has been consistently evident in the slew of policy formulations as well as commitment to international ratifications, the most prominent being the UNCRC (United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child). If the expression of this intent, in forms of various laws, has translated into a reality for children on ground, is another story altogether. The change for children has been incremental, the slow pace of reform almost making you doubt the intent itself. Child labour in the decade from 2001 to 2011 has reduced at a dismal rate of 2.2%, the country still housing 33 million children under the age of 18 in labour. The number of crimes against children has increased by 500% from 2005 to 2015. Out of every 100 children in India, only 32 (less than one-third) finish school education age-appropriately.

One of the most critical aspects determining the success of any policy is the human resources dedicated to its implementation. Ironically, most of the personnel in child related schemes and laws are ad hoc or honorary. The Labour Inspector mandated to rescue and rehabilitate children in labour is primarily responsible for implementing over 32 other labour laws, which translates to shockingly poor rates of conviction in child labour cases. Under the Child Marriage law, it is the Sub-Divisional Magistrate or a Child Development Project officer who is given the additional responsibility of preventing a complex, deep seated social taboo of child marriage. Under the Juvenile Justice law, abused and traumatised children who are extremely vulnerable are at the mercy of a five honorary member Child Welfare Committee, who, many a times are inadequately trained to deal with child protection and take critical decisions about these childrenâ€™s lives.

For nearly 4 decades, CRY and its 200 partner NGOs have worked with parents and communities across 23 states to ensure sustainable change in the live of over 2 million underprivileged children.

Public provisioning for children under 6, under the present construct of ICDS, which is a universal scheme, is only able to reach 50% of the population of 164.5 million of these children. This, despite the cognisance of this age group to be the most critical and the known fact that lack of nutrition, care and education during these growing years has lifelong implications for the child. Furthermore, the targeted approach of the government for some schemes leads to limited coverage. Take for instance, the Rajiv Gandhi Scheme for Empowerment of Adolescent Girls is only implemented in 200 select districts leaving behind many other marginalised girls, who live in districts which fail to make the cut in the most â€˜criticalâ€™ list. Considering that 56% of the adolescent girls in India are anaemic and 47% are underweight, schemes such as these need to reach to all children and thus needs adequate backing.

While we dream to create an ecosystem to safeguard the rights of every last child in the country, the lack of financial memorandum makes it a far-fetched reality. Our schemes are skewed and children continue to be seen in fragments. In a country where rate of crime against children has increased by more than 15 times in the last decade, the framework of our child protection scheme is largely urban with a limited reach to children in rural areas. There is no clear convergence of different departments and no onus for a shared responsibility. The budget for POCSO (Protection of Children from Sexual Offences Act), for example, lies with Ministry of Women and Child Development, while the budget for training of important stakeholders like doctors and health professionals dealing with these children, lies out of its ambit, with no clarity on the convergence.
